The Problem

AI systems that pass every test
still fail in the real world.

This is the gap I've built my research and my company around. It's not a niche academic puzzle — it's the core trust crisis of our moment.

The gap
Lab → Deployment
Benchmark performance and real-world reliability are alarmingly uncorrelated. Most AI audits test for what's easy to measure, not what actually breaks under pressure.
The identity layer
Synthetic ≠ Fake
Generative AI has made identity verification — the bedrock of trust in digital systems — fundamentally unreliable. Current detection tools address symptoms, not the system.
The governance failure
Policies ≠ Practice
In regulated industries, the distance between what AI governance policies say and how AI is actually deployed is wide — and growing. Accountability exists mostly on paper.
Why I'm the right person
Research + Build
I've published on these failures and I'm deploying systems into them. That combination — rigorous diagnosis plus hands-on building — is rare, and it's my unfair advantage.
"I didn't stumble into this problem. I've spent years mapping exactly where and how AI systems break trust — and now I'm building the infrastructure to fix it."
This is the through-line between my company and my eventual PhD. The research gives the company depth. The company gives the research ground truth. Both are stronger for it.

Open Questions · PhD Research Direction

What I still
don't know

These are the questions my company surfaces that research alone can answer. They're also the core of my PhD research statement.

01
Why do AI systems that pass every benchmark still fail in deployment — and can we build evaluations that predict this before it happens?
Running TrustAI Toolkit across real deployments shows consistent, reproducible gaps between lab performance and production behaviour. I want to formally characterise and predict this.
LLM EvaluationDeployment Gap
02
Does provenance infrastructure actually restore epistemic trust after synthetic media exposure — or does it address the wrong layer of the problem?
Detection accuracy is a solvable engineering problem. Whether people actually update their trust based on provenance signals is a cognitive and social question that my current research hasn't answered.
Digital IdentityCognitive Science
03
What does meaningful human oversight of AI actually look like — and how do we distinguish it from the performance of oversight?
Working with enterprise clients shows that "human-in-the-loop" often means nominal oversight while decisions are effectively automated. I want a framework that can measure the difference.
Human-AI TeamingHCI
04
Which AI governance mechanisms in regulated industries actually change deployment behaviour — and which ones exist only to satisfy audit requirements?
Interviews with AI governance leads across healthcare and finance reveal a consistent pattern: accountability documents are thorough; accountability practices are sparse. I want to explain why and what works instead.
AI GovernanceOrganisations
These questions are too large for a company alone to answer, and too applied for a lab alone to answer. That's exactly why I'm doing both. The PhD will give me the time, collaborators, and methodological depth to go after them properly.

The Story

Why both?

I didn't plan to be a founder. I planned to be a researcher. But the closer I got to the real-world failures of AI systems, the more obvious it became that waiting for a paper to publish wasn't fast enough.

So I started building — not instead of researching, but because of it. Every deployment I run teaches me something that would take years to learn from a lab study. Every interview with a governance lead surfaces a research question I couldn't have written from the inside.

The PhD is the same logic, one step further. The questions my company is surfacing are bigger than a startup can systematically answer. I want to do them justice. Fall 2027 is when I plan to go back, with two years of deployment data and a much sharper set of questions than I started with.

Both paths are about the same thing: understanding — deeply and honestly — when and why AI systems break human trust, and what it actually takes to fix it.
